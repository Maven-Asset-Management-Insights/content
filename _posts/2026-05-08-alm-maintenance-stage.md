Of the five stages of asset lifecycle management, maintenance is by far the longest. It's also where the gap between what organizations intend and what they actually do is widest.

## Scheduled maintenance is a starting point, not a strategy.

A PM program keeps assets serviced. It doesn't tell you whether the intervals are right, whether failures are recurring for a reason, or whether the maintenance cost trajectory is sustainable. Those questions require something more: condition-based monitoring, failure pattern analysis, and a feedback loop between what happens in the field and how the maintenance strategy evolves.

Modern EAM platforms like Maximo make that feedback loop possible — IoT sensor integration, predictive analytics, historical failure data all in one environment. But the technology only works when the data feeding it is structured and trustworthy.

That means consistent failure coding. Accurate work order closeout. Job plans that reflect how work is actually performed. Without that foundation, predictive maintenance becomes predictive guessing.

The maintenance stage is long enough that small discipline gaps compound into major reliability problems. Build the foundation first.
