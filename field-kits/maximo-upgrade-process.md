  <section class="card">
    <h2>1) Assess Current State</h2>
    <p>
      Establish a fact base before decisions are made. Capture technical realities and operational realities—not opinions.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Platform baseline:</strong> version, modules, environments, integrations, interfaces, security model</li>
      <li><strong>Operational baseline:</strong> planned vs. unplanned work, backlog health, critical workflows, reporting dependencies</li>
      <li><strong>Configuration footprint:</strong> customizations, automations, escalations, scripts, add-ons</li>
      <li><strong>Risk inventory:</strong> downtime sensitivity, regulatory impact, seasonal constraints</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> a clear readiness picture and risk profile to guide scope and sequencing.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>2) Establish Upgrade Goals</h2>
    <p>
      Define what “success” means in business terms. Upgrades that are justified only by “getting current” tend to underperform.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Business outcomes:</strong> reduce downtime, improve planning discipline, strengthen compliance, enable mobile, improve analytics</li>
      <li><strong>Scope intent:</strong> technical upgrade only vs. upgrade + workflow improvements</li>
      <li><strong>Success measures:</strong> adoption metrics, data quality measures, performance KPIs, reliability outcomes</li>
      <li><strong>Decision ownership:</strong> establish who signs off on scope, risk, and go-live readiness</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> a goals statement and success criteria that align all stakeholders.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>3) Develop a Plan</h2>
    <p>
      Convert objectives into a controllable plan—sequenced, resourced, and governed. Plan for testing and change management early.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Delivery approach:</strong> big-bang vs. phased; environment strategy; cutover strategy</li>
      <li><strong>Workstreams:</strong> technical, integration, data, security, reporting, training/change</li>
      <li><strong>Governance:</strong> steering cadence, decision log, scope control, risk management</li>
      <li><strong>Quality gates:</strong> clear entry/exit criteria for build, test, UAT, go-live</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> a plan that is measurable, auditable, and built for predictable execution.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>4) Upgrade Configurations and Integrations</h2>
    <p>
      Protect business continuity by deliberately managing what changes—and what must not break. Integration failures are a common
      upgrade risk multiplier.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Configuration migration:</strong> validate what will move cleanly, what must be rebuilt, what should be retired</li>
      <li><strong>Integration readiness:</strong> interface compatibility, error handling, scheduling, performance</li>
      <li><strong>Security & roles:</strong> confirm access models, segregation of duties, and audit expectations</li>
      <li><strong>Reporting dependencies:</strong> downstream consumers, BI tools, extracts, regulatory reports</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> a controlled build with reduced risk of downstream disruption.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>5) Conduct Testing</h2>
    <p>
      Testing is where leadership buys down risk. Focus on real workflows and failure scenarios—not just happy-path clicks.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Process-based testing:</strong> end-to-end scenarios for critical work (planning → execution → closeout)</li>
      <li><strong>Integration testing:</strong> validate triggers, timing, failures, and data integrity across systems</li>
      <li><strong>Performance validation:</strong> response time, batch jobs, high-volume transactions</li>
      <li><strong>Cutover rehearsal:</strong> timing, rollback plan, stakeholder communications</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> confidence that go-live will protect operations and compliance.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>6) Provide Training</h2>
    <p>
      Adoption is the difference between a “successful upgrade” and meaningful business value. Training should be role-based and operational.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Role-based enablement:</strong> planners, supervisors, technicians, storeroom, reliability, leadership</li>
      <li><strong>Workflow reinforcement:</strong> what changed, why it matters, how success will be measured</li>
      <li><strong>Job aids:</strong> short guides for critical transactions and new features</li>
      <li><strong>Support model:</strong> hypercare plan, escalation paths, feedback loop</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> higher adoption, fewer workarounds, and faster stabilization.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>7) Complete Your Upgrade</h2>
    <p>
      Go-live should be treated as a controlled operational transition, with decision clarity and measurable readiness gates.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Readiness review:</strong> sign-offs against criteria for testing, training, data, integration, and cutover</li>
      <li><strong>Hypercare:</strong> rapid triage, daily check-ins, defect prioritization, communication cadence</li>
      <li><strong>Stabilization metrics:</strong> incident rates, cycle times, backlog trends, user adoption indicators</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> controlled cutover with operational stability and transparent risk management.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>8) Drive Continuous Improvement</h2>
    <p>
      The real value is captured after go-live. Use the upgrade as the starting point for stronger reliability execution and governance.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Backlog of value:</strong> improvements and optimizations identified during upgrade work</li>
      <li><strong>Governance:</strong> data ownership, standards, KPI cadence, and accountability</li>
      <li><strong>Reliability maturity:</strong> improve failure coding, PM effectiveness, planning discipline, analytics adoption</li>
      <li><strong>Release strategy:</strong> establish a sustainable approach to ongoing Maximo changes</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Executive output:</strong> Maximo evolves into a performance platform—not a maintained system.</p>
  </section>

</div>

<p class="section-title">Leadership Takeaways</p>

<div class="card">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Anchor upgrades to business outcomes</strong> to avoid “technical success / operational disappointment.”</li>
    <li><strong>Make testing and training leadership priorities</strong> because they are the primary levers for risk and adoption.</li>
    <li><strong>Use go-live as a transition, not a finish line</strong>—continuous improvement is where value compounds.</li>
  </ul>
